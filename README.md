## Reflection on Essential Questions
### How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?
Interpreting user needs begins with active listening and asking the right questions to understand not just what users say they want, but what problems they're actually trying to solve. User stories are invaluable in this process because they frame requirements from the user's perspective using the format "As a [type of user], I want [goal] so that [reason]." This structure keeps the focus on delivering genuine value rather than just building features. User stories also include acceptance criteria that provide clear, testable conditions for when a feature is complete, which helps bridge the gap between user needs and technical implementation. Throughout this course, I learned that breaking down large requirements into smaller, manageable user stories makes it easier to prioritize work, estimate effort accurately, and deliver incremental value to users in each sprint.
### How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?
My approach to developing programs now centers on iterative development, continuous feedback, and adaptability. Rather than attempting to build everything at once with a fixed plan, I break work into sprints that deliver working software incrementally. I hope to incorporate several key Agile processes into my future work: **daily stand-ups** to maintain team communication and quickly identify blockers, **sprint planning** to ensure clear goals and realistic commitments, **sprint reviews** to demonstrate work to stakeholders and gather feedback, and **retrospectives** to continuously improve team processes. I also value the principle of maintaining a sustainable pace and being responsive to change—understanding that requirements will evolve as we learn more about user needs and market conditions. Test-driven development and continuous integration are technical practices I aim to adopt to ensure code quality while maintaining development velocity.
Being a good team member in software development means much more than writing good code. It requires clear and proactive communication, including being transparent about progress, challenges, and blockers. A good team member actively participates in ceremonies like stand-ups and retrospectives, offering constructive feedback and being open to receiving it. Collaboration is essential—this means helping teammates when they're stuck, conducting thorough code reviews, and sharing knowledge rather than hoarding it. Good team members also take collective ownership of the product, understanding that the team succeeds or fails together. This includes being reliable with commitments, respecting the Definition of Done, and prioritizing the team's goals over individual preferences. Finally, a good team member demonstrates adaptability and a growth mindset, embracing change and continuously seeking ways to improve both personally and as part of the team. Throughout this course, I learned that the most effective Scrum teams are built on trust, respect, and a shared commitment to delivering value to users.
